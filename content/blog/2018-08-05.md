# Interesting links I found today

## EA

I was linked to a discussion about representation of the EA community ([here](http://effective-altruism.com/ea/1r5/problems_with_ea_representativeness_and_how_to/))  by [@kfs](https://mastodon.host/@kfs), which for me held [this gem](http://effective-altruism.com/ea/1r5/problems_with_ea_representativeness_and_how_to/f59) of a summary on why some people (including me) don't buy the arguments offered by e.g.  [80khours](https://80000hours.org/articles/future-generations/) for why the *far* future (as in , thousands or millions of years) should more weight in our considerations than the near future.
I am actually working on a short post on that topic, trying to convey intuitively the impact even small discount factors have (and why we should use them when thinking about altruism analytically). The text right now is a bit snarky and needs rework, since I was getting quite frustrated with what discourse I had seen so far online when I was originally writing it. This comment and the links it included  made it clear to me that I can be a bit less tough on the online EA crowd in this regard, which makes me quite happy.

## Hackernews gems


* [Heated discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=17689683) over an article pushing a paper arguing against the acceptance of loss aversion in the scientific community. I found the paper interesting,(it's linked in the HN discussion) but not fully convinced by the arguments...but then, that is exactly that the last part [of the paper] which concerns itself with status qup bias in scientific consensus expects.
* [Completely unrelated](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=17688336) discussion about sci-hub. I  pretty much agree. It is an outrage that we might pay three for a paper (with taxes to pay the author, with taxes to allow the university to pay for journal access so he or she can write that paper and finally ourselves to read it). Publishers need to provide a much more significant value add than currently to justify the price or die already, and that at least basic access to the paper should be free. I read an idea of giving additional state funding to the science societies in exchange for them editing and curating journals. That seems like a good deal, then we can use the parts of university budgets which are currently going to Elsevier et. al. 
* [A discussion of Ai Wei's recent troubles in China](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=17689293), with not one but two commenters (at time of writing) arguing that PRC authorities and Wei might be in some sort of symbiotic-hate relationship
* [Vectorization for Arrow](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=17688140)...I like Arrow and JITs
* [Decentralized archive.org](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=17685682), with discussion of the problems of decentralization. I donate to archive.org and like decentralization. I think having some sort of *permament* memory where information can't be completely hidden would be overall valuable. But I see BIG problems with this immutable record as well. I wonder how to deal with stuff like revenge porn, instigations to violence and other problematic stuff...
One way might be to seal things away for a (renewable) time but have the seal still be public+a way to put context over/onto texts (e.g. holocaust denial corrections inserted into the text with the original text only visible in a diff view) would be good overall I think. And maybe a consens based deletion (which because consens means close to 100% should almost never be used). The last bit is either hard (for sybill attacks etc) or trivial to implement though (by taking all of the nodes hosting some specific data offline).
